The Working Group is interested in understanding how to best apply technology to humanistic problems. Big Data methods are the fashion at the moment, but it's useful to ask how to do things properly, and what mistakes people have made in the past. It's also an awareness problem how do you learn about possible services/technologies, or what problems/questions are out there to be solved?

One of the project examples is Cendari, which is an infrastructure for research into archives. Cendari was focused on permitting scholars to investigate across multiple archives, in multiple locations, with a focus on the *hidden* and thw *under-resourced*. Two test cases were chosen: WW1, and Medieval Manuscripts. Cendari was recently launched, having reached the end of its development with EU funding.

Cendari took a user-centric approach to design, and focused on the creation of tools which fit the existing workflows of scholars, and facilitated the federation of archival holdings without duplication. The nature of the project meant that it had complex data requirements, ranging from handling archive holdings to user-created notes.

One end of the data ingestion for Cendari was via archive holdings. There was considerablue variaiton in what and how archives stored their information. Holdings were made available in custom XML, in MODS, EAD, and countless other standards. Ultimately the project settled on 10 formats to accept. Transporting the files ranged from OAI-PMH to FTP. You can see the details of the findings of the project on Archives and contributing to Cendari at the link.

The Second type of ingestion is via the user. Cendari unites institutional information and user knowledge via a research workspace. These research workspaces can be personal, private places were scholars upload, collate and annotate information, or they can be the basis for public, shared Archive Research guides: Collaborative, themeatic research guides to material. A key feature of the NTE is the ability to work on entities ('Strings, not things'), and the Cendari infrastructure leverages ontological information for that.

Named Entity Recognition brings together all of the challenges that were faced in CENDARI, and are representative of the knowledge challenge facing much TDA. How do you recognise references to (people, plaves, times, events)? How do you know which specific one is being referenced? What if it's not been seen before? NERD Demo NERD Leverages online knowledge bases, open corpora, for 26 entity classes. A REST API is under active development.
